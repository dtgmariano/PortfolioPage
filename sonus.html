DESIGN CHALLENGE

BRIEFING

Jam sessions are a nice social activities where people such as young adults do not only show their musical skills, but also produce music together and get a connected feeling with each other that can enhance the group dynamics. A music illiterate in the group however would feel left out in this scenario. That person could maybe only tap to the beat, or play the tambourine, also to mimic the beat, but thatâ€™s where it stops. The tambourine does not always fit the sound that the band is playing and it can quite a monotonous interaction with the instrument. To solve this problem we aim to design a personalised musical instrument to enable young adults who are music illiterates in friendly social gatherings to produce meaningful sounds in order to express themselves artistically.

TANGIBLE PROTOTYPE

WHAT WE DID

The product is a handheld device that provides haptic direct manipulation as there are different elements on the product, such as the volume, the knob and the different sound modules. It does not provide haptic feedback however, but rather visual (light) and auditorial feedback. Sonus provides an active experience as the user can use small movements by hand and big arm movements to control the music, and naturally move the entire body to engage with the music (Spatial Interaction). The user has to explore which movement belongs to which sound effect (performative action). The interaction with Sonus is lightweight (Tangible Manipulation) as it has a low learning curve. The user has a low threshold to explore the functionalities as the sound immediately reacts to the movement.

As described in the MCRpd interaction model, there are three main key characteristics of tangible interfaces. First, there is the mental model. In the concept of Sonus, the usersâ€™ movement guide the music. Normally, you should press a key or touch a string to produce a sound but only experienced musicians can relate notes to corresponding keys or strings. Sonus links the movement directly to the music. The tempo that you move the device, determines the tempo of the music. Second, control of the device. Sonus uses physical representations for control. The user can add an instrument by attaching a sound module to the device. In this way the user has a clear overview of the instruments he or she is controlling. Finally, the link between physical and digital representations. For this concept the digital respresentation is the sound and the light that is the output of the interaction with the device.
